Overview

This data science project, conducted at UC San Diego, explores the relationship between the cooking time of a recipe and its average user rating. Recipes differ widely in how long they take to prepare, and understanding whether users prefer longer or shorter recipes can provide insight into user behavior and recipe design.

Introduction

Food is an important part of everyday life, and cooking is both a necessity and a source of enjoyment for many people. At the same time, time is often a major factor in deciding what to cook. Some users may prefer quick and convenient recipes, while others may be more willing to spend extra time preparing a meal if the results are better. Because of this, we want to investigate whether cooking time is related to how highly a recipe is rated. More specifically, we ask: do recipes that take longer to prepare receive higher average ratings, or do users tend to prefer recipes with shorter cooking times?

To answer this question, we analyze two datasets containing recipes and user interactions from Food.com. These datasets were originally collected for recommender system research in the paper Generating Personalized Recipes from Historical User Preferences by Majumder et al.

The first dataset, RAW_recipes, contains 83,782 rows, representing 83,782 unique recipes, with columns describing recipe characteristics:

Column Description
nameRecipe name
idRecipe ID
minutesMinutes required to prepare the recipe
contributor_idUser ID of the person who submitted the recipe
submittedDate the recipe was submitted
tagsFood.com tags associated with the recipe
nutrition Nutrition information in the form [calories, total fat, sugar, sodium, protein, saturated fat, carbohydrates] in PDV format
n_stepsNumber of steps in the recipe
stepsText of the recipe instructions, in order
descriptionUser-provided recipe description
ingredientsList of recipe ingredients
n_ingredientsNumber of ingredients in the recipe

The second dataset, interactions, contains 731,927 rows. Each row represents a user interaction with a specific recipe:

Column Description
user_idUser ID
recipe_idRecipe ID
dateDate of interaction
ratingRating given by the user
reviewReview text

Given these datasets, we investigate whether recipes with different cooking times receive different ratings. To support this analysis, we create a new variable, avg_rating, which represents the average rating of each recipe. We first treat ratings of 0 as missing values, since they do not represent valid ratings. Then, we group the interactions dataset by recipe_id, compute the mean rating for each recipe, and merge those averages into the recipes dataset.

The most relevant columns for answering our question are minutes, which gives the total cooking or preparation time of a recipe; rating, which is the score given by a user; avg_rating, the derived average rating for each recipe; and id / recipe_id, which allow the two datasets to be merged.

By studying this relationship, we hope to better understand how time investment affects user satisfaction. This may help users choose recipes more effectively, help recipe writers understand audience preferences, and provide useful insight for recommendation systems built around online recipe platforms.
